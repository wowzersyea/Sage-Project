New ESPID Guidelines on Complicated Urinary Tract Infections in Children
Pediatric Infectious Disease Journal, February 2026 | https://doi.org/10.1097/INF.0000000000004687
PDF: Available via PMC (Open Access)

  • Access: OPEN ACCESS

  • The European Society for Pediatric Infectious Diseases has published the first comprehensive guidelines specifically addressing complicated urinary tract infections (cUTI) in children, filling a significant gap in pediatric management recommendations

  • Key Recommendations: cUTI is defined as UTI in children with increased risk of failing conventional management, categorized into five subgroups: localized risk (anatomical/functional abnormalities, recurrent UTI), generalized risk (neonates, underlying conditions), and severe presentations (sepsis, renal disease)

  • Clinical Impact: For most children with cUTI, trial oral antibiotics initially unless severely unwell, with IV therapy reserved for significant abnormalities (VUR grades IV-V), sepsis, renal abscesses, or neonates <2 months

  • Evidence Grade: Multiple GRADE recommendations from A to D based on systematic literature review through December 2024

Antibiotics, October 2025 | https://doi.org/10.3390/antibiotics14101011
PDF: https://www.mdpi.com/2079-6382/14/10/1011/pdf

  • Access: OPEN ACCESS

  • Design: Four-round modified Delphi consensus process with experts in antimicrobial stewardship and infectious diseases to identify priority pediatric ASP interventions

  • Methods: Minimum 20 experts recruited based on existing studies suggesting saturation with 11-50 participants, conducted February 24-26, 2025

  • Key Findings: Seven key priorities clustered into three themes: Care Settings (outpatient clinics, ICUs), Prescriptions (shorter durations, narrow-spectrum agents), and Strategies (outcome-based metrics, diagnostic stewardship, routine outcome tracking)

  • Limitations: Single geographic region, expert consensus methodology

  • Clinical Implications: Provides foundation for future pediatric ASP interventions and can inform policy to improve appropriate antimicrobial use and combat resistance

Management of Serious Staphylococcus aureus Infections in Children: Canadian Survey
Paediatrics & Child Health, Advance Online February 8, 2026 | https://doi.org/10.1093/pch/pxaf122
PDF: https://academic.oup.com/pch/advance-article-pdf/doi/10.1093/pch/pxaf122/66634273/pxaf122.pdf

  • Access: OPEN ACCESS

  • Design: Survey of Canadian pediatric infectious diseases physicians regarding contemporary management practices for serious Staphylococcus aureus infections in children

  • Methods: Survey disseminated via PICNIC site representatives at 14 Canadian pediatric hospitals to pediatric ID physicians

  • Key Findings: For MSSA infections outside CNS, 56.4% preferred cefazolin, 26.3% used ceftriaxone/cefotaxime, and 51.3% chose penicillin for penicillin-sensitive SA

  • Treatment Duration: Most respondents treated SA bacteremia with no focus for 14 days in immunocompetent children (89.7%) and neonates (92.3%)

  • Practice Variability: Significant variability in ordering imaging for SAB and transitioning to oral antibiotics for MSSA bacteremia, with 56.4% choosing IV antibiotics until clinical response for MRSA osteoarticular infections

  • Clinical Implications: Study highlights need for evidence-based pediatric guidelines and increased inclusion of children in clinical trials

Severe Group A Streptococcus Infections in French Children
Emerging Infectious Diseases, February 2026 | CDC

  • Access: OPEN ACCESS

  • Design: Retrospective and prospective study by French Pediatric Intensive Care and Pediatric Infectious Diseases groups across 34 hospitals, September 2022-April 2024, enrolling 402 pediatric patients (median age 4 years)

  • Key Findings: Low proportion of severe skin/soft tissue infections (16%), predominance of respiratory tract infections (55%), 3.5% case-fatality rate, with hydrocortisone and vasopressor therapy associated with major sequelae or death

  • Molecular Analysis: emm1 strains predominant (73.0%), emm12 (10.8%), with M1UK clone representing 50% of emm1 strains, emphasizing need for collaborative public health response

Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever in Mexican Children: 20-Year Analysis
Emerging Infectious Diseases, February 2026 | CDC
  • Design: Retrospective investigation of 500 pediatric RMSF patients hospitalized in Sonora, 2004-2024, analyzing clinical and sociodemographic predictors of fatality

  • Key Findings: Overall case-fatality rate 19.8%, decreasing from 31.4% (2004-2013) to 14.5% (2014-2024); delayed doxycycline treatment (>5 days) associated with fatal outcomes; 16% of survivors had life-altering sequelae
